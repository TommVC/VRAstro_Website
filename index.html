VRAstro - Selection in VR

Data visualisation and selection is a complex topic. Most tools for achieving this make use of a desktop environment. This project aimed to determine whether virtual reality offers a better environment for sub-volume selection of volumetric datasets. Some examples of these datasets, visualised as data cubes, are displayed to the right.

Overview

Data comes in many forms depending on the context it is used for. For example, astronomers collect three dimensional radio wave data of our universe. This takes a two-dimensional snapshot of some region of the universe, as well as capturing some spectral data across this snapshot. Hence, these snapshots have three dimensions of data attatched to them: two spatial, and one spectral. The astronomy department at the Univeristy of Cape Town has developed virtual reality software, iDaVIE-v, that allows astronomers to visualise and analyze the radio wave data mentioned previously. Our goal with this project was to use and extend this software to develop ways to select out sub-volumes of data from these datasets with virtual reality. In addition to this, we developed a method of selection using a desktop environment to use as a baseline for comparison to determine whether virtual reality offers a better environment for selection.

Experiment

Experimental Design

Goal: to determine whether virtual reality offers a better environment for sub-volume selection of volumetric data sets compared to a desktop environment.

Our experiment consisted of gathering thirty users to test each of the methods with different selection tasks. Users used each of the four methods, in varying orders, completing different selection tasks with each. We measured various metrics throughout each experiment to allow us to compare the methods of selection in hopes of determining whether the VR methods improved upon the desktop method.

Metrics

Accuracy: the users in the test were shown an ideal selection to be made from the data cube they then did their best to recreate that selection with a given method. Their result was measured against the ideal selection they were shown using F1 Score and MCC to produce accuracy values.

Efficiency: We recorded the time it took a user to finish each task, they had a maximum of 10 minutes per task.

Workload: a Task Load Index (TLX) survey was filled out by the user after each task to assess the workload of the task they just completed.

Usability: A System Usability Scale (SUS) survey was also filled out after each task, to gauge the usability of the selection method used to complete the task.

Conclusions

The results from the experiment show that there is no significant improvement in VR selection in terms of accuracy, efficiency, usability and workload. However, there are many factors that contributed to this, such as users needing to learn how to navigate in VR compared to intuitive desktop controls. While no results were significant, some metrics showed slightly higher averages and less spread for accuracy and fatigue in VR, hinting that further experimentation could reveal more accurate and realistic results.
